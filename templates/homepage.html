IV-Safe

Approximately 90% of patients in the United States receive PIV therapy during their hospital stays. And yet 50% of all IVs fail. The burden is even greater with young patients: failure is greater than 90% in neonates and greater than 60% in children.
When IV infilration occurs, the administered fluid begins to leak out of the vein and into the surrounding tissue. Minor infiltration events lead to swelling, blistering, and pain. However, severe cases can lead to tissue necrosis, amputation, and even death.
Currently, IV infiltration is detected by visually by nurses, or by the patient alerting the medical staff of discomfort. Nurses can easily miss infiltrations due to limited staffing, limited time with the patient, or poor lighting. However, children and neonates may not be able to communicate adequately, and thus IV infiltration may go unnoticed for prolonged periods and increase the risk of complications and severe injury.
